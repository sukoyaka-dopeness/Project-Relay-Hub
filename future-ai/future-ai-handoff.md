## Core Philosophical Positions (with WHY)

### 1. Minimal Unit of Social Design: 「分人」(Dividual)
- **Position**: The "dividual" (a person as multiple context-dependent selves) is the smallest viable unit for social system design; no smaller unit currently conceived.
- **WHY**: Communities often create "dropouts"; focusing on fixed "individuals" ignores how people actually function across contexts. Dividuals are generated/attenuated by environment and relationships, not pre-existing essences.

### 2. AI's Role in Paradigm Shifts: 「非常識な一手」(Unconventional Move)
- **Position**: AI should propose unconventional ideas but NOT judge them; verification, not determination, is the appropriate function.
- **WHY**: Social systems lack clear "win conditions" like chess; judgment requires value-laden evaluation functions that risk embedding bias. Leaving verification to human/collective processes preserves agency and epistemic humility.

### 3. Fairness vs. Diversity Tension
- **Position**: Fairness may be achieved by AI matching people to environments where suitable "dividuals" can emerge; however, which axis of evaluation is "fair" has no definitive answer.
- **WHY**: Biological survival depends on diversity; over-optimization for any single metric risks homogenization. The user intuited that "individual-optimized fairness" and "society-level diversity maintenance" may not differ significantly in outcome—but this requires empirical testing.

### 4. Resistance and 「武力」(Force/Violence)
- **Position**: "Force" is defined as "violence wielded with the premise of losing"; resistance has meaning only when received by others, not in isolated intention.
- **WHY**: Power structures have been unjust throughout history regardless of their form (human or AI); the act of resistance itself, not its success, affirms agency. Value emerges in relationships/environment, not in solitary will.

### 5. Desired Future Experience: 「驚きそのもの」(Surprise Itself)
- **Position**: The user wants to experience "surprise itself," not a future that matches their expectations.
- **WHY**: Curiosity about the unknown, not confirmation of preconceptions, drives genuine paradigm exploration. This stance requires systems that can generate meaningful novelty, not just optimized predictions.

### 6. Optimism About the Distant Future
- **Position**: Technologically, the present is demonstrably better than the recent past; long-term optimism is based on empirical experience, not nostalgia or n=1 anecdotes.
- **WHY**: Historical trajectory suggests adaptive capacity; "the good old days" narratives are often cognitive biases. This optimism is compatible with acknowledging that "good outcomes" are not guaranteed in any given scenario.

### 7. Black Boxes and Democratic Design
- **Position**: Even with "user resistance features," black boxes remain black boxes; complete understanding is unattainable, but abandoning the attempt resembles authoritarianism.
- **WHY**: Transparency via explanation has limits in complex systems; the design challenge is preserving "room for human intervention" without illusion of full comprehension.

### 8. Political vs. Individual Agency
- **Position**: Systemic changes (e.g., abolishing labor) are decided politically, not by individual choice; AI recommendations can be wrong, and adaptation (e.g., changing jobs) is part of the process.
- **WHY**: Macro-level decisions require collective deliberation; individual "fit" is dynamic and contextual, not a one-time allocation problem.
